# My assessment of the GPT-5.6 (High) review and corrected patch proposal

I authored the three P0 patch sketches GPT-5.6 reviewed, so I read its critique adversarially and
re-verified its load-bearing new claims against source (`08fb47373`, current `dev`). Bottom line:

**I largely agree with GPT-5.6. Its review is stronger and more complete than my patch sketches, it
caught two real bugs I missed, and its "requires redesign / split" verdicts are correct.** I have a few
refinements and one process caution, below. I checked its new claims rather than deferring.

## Where GPT-5.6 is right and I was wrong (verified)

1. **Patch 3 was incomplete — `web.ts` also fails open, independently.** VERIFIED:
   `packages/opencode/src/cli/cmd/web.ts:40-44` has its own missing-password warning (via `UI.println`,
   distinct from `serve.ts`'s `console.log`) and proceeds to `Server.listen`. My `serve.ts`-only patch
   would have left `web` open. GPT-5.6's "shared startup policy across every listener path" is the
   correct design, not a nicety.

2. **Patch 2 has an interruptibility bug I did not catch.** VERIFIED: `shellImpl` is wrapped in
   `Effect.uninterruptibleMask((restore) => ...)` (`prompt.ts:452`), and `restore` is only applied at the
   actual spawn (~`prompt.ts:553`). My proposed `permission.ask` insertion sits in the inner block
   *before* that, i.e. **inside the uninterruptible region with no `restore`**. A blocking permission
   wait there would be non-interruptible — HTTP cancel/shutdown couldn't clean it up. GPT-5.6 is correct;
   the wait must be wrapped in `restore(...)` and bounded. This is a genuine defect in my sketch.

3. **Patch 2's no-responder behavior is a merge blocker, not a footnote.** I flagged the hang as a
   "design decision"; GPT-5.6 correctly elevates it: a naive `ask` on a headless/bare `serve` hangs
   indefinitely, retains fibers/HTTP state, and can block shutdown. Its policy — attended-TUI may prompt,
   remote-API fails fast (403) unless pre-approved — is the right resolution and should gate the merge.

4. **Patch 2's `always: [command]` and `Effect.orDie` are both wrong.** Agreed on both. Using the raw
   command as the remembered-approval pattern diverges from the shell tool's normalized planner
   (`tool/shell.ts`), degrading "always allow"; and `orDie` converts an expected denial into a defect
   instead of a 403. GPT-5.6's "factor a shared `ShellPermission.plan()` reused by tool + endpoint" is
   the correct fix and better than my inline call.

5. **Patch 1: my `SECRET_KEYS` blacklist is structurally incomplete.** Agreed. Provider `options` and
   `headers` are extensible (`Record<string,string>`), so a finite name list can't be a durable secret
   boundary. GPT-5.6's independent test retaining `ApiKey`/`api_key`/`X-Api-Key`/`httpHeaders.authorization`
   is a fair demonstration. A schema-defined public DTO (write-only secret fields) is the honest fix; my
   redactor is at best an emergency stopgap. Its case/dash/underscore normalization and
   `Proxy-Authorization`/cookie coverage are real improvements.

I concede all of the above. The triple-check I did earlier caught the field-shape and scope bugs but
missed the interruptibility issue and the `web.ts` gap — both of which GPT-5.6 found.

## Where I'd refine or push back (minor)

- **Cycle handling in Patch 1 is likely a non-issue, and GPT-5.6 half-says so.** `ConfigV1.Info` is
  schema-decoded JSON data (it round-trips through `JSON.parse`/JSON responses), so cycles cannot occur
  in practice. My `WeakMap` cycle guard was defensive over-engineering, not a bug. GPT-5.6's framing
  ("either cycles are impossible and the machinery is unnecessary, or they're possible and JSON already
  fails") is correct — the practical answer is "impossible; drop the machinery and just omit secret
  fields on a schema projection." So this simplifies rather than complicates the fix. Agreement, with the
  emphasis that it argues *for* the simpler DTO approach.

- **`Effect.orDie` nuance:** GPT-5.6 lists this as "plausible but unverified against HTTP error layers."
  Confirming for the record: in my sketch `orDie` was applied to the `permission.ask` call, so yes it
  would turn a deny/reject into a defect. Its concern is valid, not just plausible — treat it as
  confirmed.

- **Severity framing is fair.** GPT-5.6 does not overclaim: it correctly narrows Issue A ("a listener
  must exist"), narrows Issue D ("caller-selected root, not traversal"), and rates the timing-safe
  compare as Low. I have no objection to its severity table. If anything it is slightly more
  conservative than the advisory, which is appropriate for a third-party review.

- **One thing neither of us fully closed:** the `SessionPrompt.command` / `ConfigMarkdown.shell(...)`
  path. GPT-5.6 marks it "partially confirmed" and a merge blocker for any completeness claim. I agree
  and can trace it fully on request — it should be resolved before asserting Patch 2 closes the
  command-exec surface.
